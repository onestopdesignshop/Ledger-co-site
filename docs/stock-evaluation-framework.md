## The Five Questions We Ask About Any Stock

### 1. What does this company actually do, in one sentence?

If you can’t explain the business simply, you don’t understand it well enough to own it. “They make a chip that does X for Y customers” is a sentence. “Revolutionary AI-powered blockchain synergy platform” is not.

### 2. Is it making money — and is that improving or worsening?

Look at revenue growth *and* whether margins are expanding or shrinking. A company growing revenue 30%/year while losing more money every year is a very different bet than one growing 10%/year while becoming more profitable.

### 3. Who’s on the other side of the trade, and why?

If a stock just moved sharply, ask what changed. Earnings? News? Or just momentum and social media attention? Price action driven by fundamentals behaves differently than price action driven by hype — and only one of those tends to last.

### 4. What would have to be true for this to fail?

Every company has a bear case. Write it down in one or two sentences before you buy. If you can’t articulate a real risk, you haven’t looked hard enough — you’ve just gotten excited.

### 5. What’s your actual exit plan — before you enter, not after?

Decide in advance: at what point would you sell because you were *right*, and at what point would you sell because you were *wrong*? Writing this down before you buy removes the emotion that wrecks most individual investors’ returns — panic-selling at the bottom, or greed-holding past the top.

## A Red Flag Checklist (the pattern-level version)

Watch for these patterns when evaluating any opportunity, not just stocks:

- **Urgency without a reason.** “Act now” language with no specific catalyst is a sales tactic, not new information.
- **Guaranteed or “near-certain” returns.** No legitimate investment can promise this — markets don’t work that way.
- **Complexity used to impress rather than inform.** If the explanation requires you to just trust the person rather than understand the mechanism, that’s a problem.
- **Pressure to skip your own research “because there’s no time.”** Real opportunities can survive 24–48 hours of you doing basic diligence.

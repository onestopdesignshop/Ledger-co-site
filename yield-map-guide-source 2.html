Where Things Stand Right Now

The Federal Reserve has held its benchmark federal funds rate steady at 3.50%–3.75% through the first half of 2026, after three rate cuts in late 2025. That pause means savings and CD rates have mostly stabilized too — drifting down slightly, but nowhere near a cliff. The takeaway: the accounts below are still paying meaningfully more than a typical bank, and there's no obvious reason to wait for a "better moment" to move your cash.

For context: the FDIC's published national average savings rate is just 0.38% APY. Every account in this guide pays at least 8–13x that. If your money is sitting in a traditional bank's default savings account, this is the single highest-leverage 15-minute task in your financial life this month.

The Fee & Minimum-Balance Traps (Read This Before You Apply)

Almost every "top APY" headline has an asterisk. Here are the three patterns that show up over and over:

1. The Balance Cap

Varo's 5.00% sounds like the obvious winner — until you realize it only applies up to $5,000. If you're depositing $20,000, the blended effective rate on that account is much lower than 5.00%, because everything above the cap earns far less. Always ask: "what does my fifteenth thousand dollars earn here?"

2. The Tiered Minimum

CIT Platinum Savings is the clearest example: $5,000+ gets you a strong rate; anything less gets you roughly 0.25% — barely better than a traditional bank. If you're not consistently going to hold the minimum, a flat-rate account (like Pibank or American Express) is usually the better real-world choice, even if its top-line number is slightly lower.

3. The Disappearing Promo

Western Alliance's 3.80% through Raisin, and various "boosted rate" offers, are time-limited. They're genuinely good while they last — just calendar the end date the moment you open the account, so you're not surprised when it quietly reverts to a lower standard rate.

The one-question test: before opening any account from a "top rate" list, ask: "Is this rate guaranteed for my actual balance, indefinitely, or is there a number or a date where it changes?" If you can't answer that from the page you're looking at, you haven't found the real terms yet — keep digging on the issuer's own site.

CDs: When They Beat a Savings Account

With the Fed on pause, top CD rates are currently running close to — and in some terms, ahead of — the best savings accounts, which is a meaningfully different situation than a year or two ago. The trade-off is simple: you lock up access to the money for the CD's term, and in exchange you usually get rate certainty a variable-rate savings account can't offer.

A Simple Way to Decide Between Savings and a CD

  • If you might need the money in the next 0–6 months: stick with a high-yield savings account. Don't lock it up.
  • If you know you won't touch it for 12+ months: a CD at today's rates is worth comparing against your savings account's rate.
  • If you're not sure: split it. Keep an emergency-fund portion liquid in savings, and ladder the rest across CD terms — see the Income-Layering Worksheet in The Annotated Portfolio for a structured way to do this with your own numbers.

Before You Open Anything: The FDIC/NCUA Check

Every account in this guide should be FDIC-insured (banks) or NCUA-insured (credit unions) up to $250,000 per depositor, per institution. This is non-negotiable for where you keep meaningful savings. Two ways to verify in under a minute:

  • Search the institution name directly on the FDIC's BankFind tool (fdic.gov) or the NCUA's Credit Union Locator (ncua.gov).
  • Look for the official FDIC or NCUA badge on the account's own application page — not just a logo in a footer, but a direct statement of insured status.

If you can't confirm insured status in those places, that's a reason to stop and ask questions before depositing anything — regardless of how good the advertised rate looks.

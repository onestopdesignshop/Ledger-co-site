Five things to know first

If you read nothing else, read this. Everything below rests on it.

1. Risk and return are joined at the hip.

Higher potential return always means a higher chance of loss. Always. Anyone offering high returns with low or no risk is either lying to you or doesn't understand their own product. There is no third option.

2. Fees are quiet and enormous.

A fee sounds small as a percentage and lands hard over time. It comes out whether you gain or lose, every single year, and it compounds against you.

3. Being able to get your money out is part of the investment.

You can be completely right about something and still be ruined by not being able to access your money when you need it. Check the exit before you check the upside.

4. You can verify almost anyone for free, in two minutes.

In the US, brokers are searchable at brokercheck.finra.org, investment advisers at adviserinfo.sec.gov, and both at investor.gov. Almost nobody checks. Checking puts you ahead of almost everybody.

5. "I don't understand it" is a complete reason to say no.

You are never obligated to invest in something you can't explain. Confusion is not a personal failing to push through — very often it is the product working as designed.

The four patterns behind most bad deals

Every question above traces back to one of these.

01

Return claims

“Guaranteed,” “risk-free,” or unusually high and steady returns. Real markets do not behave that way.

02

Transparency

You cannot get straight, written answers about how it makes money, who runs it, or where your funds go.

03

Exit & liquidity

Getting your money back out is slow, penalised, or blocked. Trouble exiting is the classic late warning.

04

Source credibility

Unregistered sellers, unsolicited outreach, urgency, secrecy, or structures that ask you to recruit others.

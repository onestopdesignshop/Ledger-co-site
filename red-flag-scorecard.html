Red Flags

Most bad investments announce themselves before they go wrong. This is what they say, why it matters, and exactly how to check — free, and in plain English.

Five things to know first

If you read nothing else, read this. Everything below rests on it.

1. Risk and return are joined at the hip.

Higher potential return always means a higher chance of loss. Always. Anyone offering high returns with low or no risk is either lying to you or doesn't understand their own product. There is no third option.

2. Fees are quiet and enormous.

A fee sounds small as a percentage and lands hard over time. It comes out whether you gain or lose, every single year, and it compounds against you.

3. Being able to get your money out is part of the investment.

You can be completely right about something and still be ruined by not being able to access your money when you need it. Check the exit before you check the upside.

4. You can verify almost anyone for free, in two minutes.

In the US, brokers are searchable at brokercheck.finra.org, investment advisers at adviserinfo.sec.gov, and both at investor.gov. Company filings are public at sec.gov/edgar. Almost nobody checks. Checking puts you ahead of almost everybody.

5. "I don't understand it" is a complete reason to say no.

You are never obligated to invest in something you can't explain. Confusion is not a personal failing to push through — very often it is the product working as designed.

The twelve warning signs

Plain English. What it means, why it matters, and the one thing to actually do.

What to do with your result

A score is only useful if it changes what you do next.

0–24 · Lower risk
Few flags. That is not the same as "safe" — it means nothing obvious is wrong.
  • Still verify the seller at investor.gov before money moves.
  • Get the total annual cost, as one number, in writing.
  • Write down, in one sentence, how this makes money. If you can't, stop.
  • Decide in advance what would make you exit — and how you'd do it.
25–49 · Caution
Real warning signs are present. This is the point where most people talk themselves into it anyway.
  • Do not send money while you are still unsure. There is no deadline worth this.
  • Put every claim they made in an email and ask them to confirm it in writing. Watch what happens.
  • Take it to one independent person who is not paid by the seller.
  • Identify which specific flags fired and get each one answered — not explained away.
50–100 · High risk
This pattern matches how most investment fraud and blowups actually look from the inside.
  • Stop sending money. Immediately, and including any fee "required to release" funds — that request is itself near-conclusive.
  • Do not tell the seller you are investigating. Fraudsters accelerate when discovered.
  • Save everything: messages, screenshots, names, wallet addresses, bank details.
  • Report it. In the US: sec.gov/tcr, reportfraud.ftc.gov, and ic3.gov for anything online. Your state securities regulator too.
  • If you have already sent money, contact your bank now. Speed matters enormously.
